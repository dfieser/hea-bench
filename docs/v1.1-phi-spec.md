## A. Background and Motivation

### A.1 The two "phi" parameters in the HEA literature

The high-entropy alloy literature contains two distinct thermodynamic parameters that are both pronounced "phi" and that are often referenced together. They are conceptually related but mathematically and numerically different. Both papers appeared in Volume 104 of sister Elsevier journals, Scripta Materialia in 2015 and Acta Materialia in 2016, a coincidence that has caused recurring confusion in downstream reviews and machine learning feature tables.

**Parameter 1. King Î¦ (capital), Acta Materialia 104 (2016) 172 to 179.**
Authors are D.J.M. King, S.C. Middleburgh, A.G. McGregor, and M.B. Cortie. The parameter is defined as the ratio of two Gibbs free energy changes computed from the binary subsystems of the multicomponent alloy:

```
Î¦ = ÎG_ss / |ÎG_max|

ÎG_ss   = ÎH_ss â T Â· ÎS_conf
ÎS_conf = âR Â· Î£_i x_i Â· ln(x_i)
ÎH_ss   = enthalpy of forming a fully disordered solid solution from
          the constituent elements, computed by the extended Miedema
          semi-empirical model across binary subsystems
ÎG_max  = the most negative (intermetallic-forming) or most positive
          (segregating) Gibbs free energy obtainable from any binary
          subsystem of the alloy at temperature T
```

The decision rule is `Î¦ â¥ 1` predicts solid solution, `Î¦ < 1` predicts intermetallic or segregated multi-phase. The model is evaluated at a user-specified temperature representing the annealing or processing condition. King and coworkers report that the criterion correctly predicts the single-phase outcome for 177 of 185 experimentally reported HEA systems in their benchmark, an accuracy of 95.68 percent. The method requires Miedema-model parameters for 73 metallic elements but does not require any new fit coefficients beyond those tabulated in the Miedema and Takeuchi and Inoue compilations that already underpin hea-bench's ÎHmix computation.

**Parameter 2. Ye Ï (lowercase), Scripta Materialia 104 (2015) 53 to 55.**
Authors are Y.F. Ye, Q. Wang, J. Lu, C.T. Liu, and Y. Yang. The parameter is defined as a single dimensionless ratio of the entropic stabilization energy to the residual enthalpic plus mismatch term:

```

T_m     = composition-weighted melting temperature
ÎS_mix  = configurational entropy of mixing, âR Â· Î£_i x_i Â· ln(x_i)
ÎH_mix  = Miedema mixing enthalpy
S_E     = excess (mismatch) entropy of mixing arising from dense
          atomic packing and atomic size misfit, computed via the
          Mansoori hard sphere model with packing fraction Î¾
          (0.68 for bcc, 0.74 for fcc)
```

The decision rule is `Ï â¥ 20` predicts a single-phase solid solution, `Ï < 20` predicts multi-phase or intermetallic. The key formal difference from the Yang and Zhang Î© parameter already in hea-bench is the inclusion of the mismatch entropy S_E in the denominator, which captures atomic size misfit effects that Î© ignores.

**The Anamu et al. 2023 JMRT context.**
The paper at Journal of Materials Research and Technology 27 (2023) 4833 to 4860 by U.S. Anamu, O.O. Ayodele, E. Olorundaisi and coauthors is a broad review article on HEA design strategies, open access under CC BY-NC-ND. It does not propose a new phi parameter. It tabulates and discusses both King's capital Î¦ and Ye's lowercase Ï alongside Î©, VEC, Î´, and other empirical criteria, presenting them as complementary screening rules. King 2016 remains the more widely cited of the two phi formulations in the primary HEA literature, in part because its binary-subsystem framing maps more directly onto computational thermodynamics workflows.

### A.2 Why a phi-style criterion is worth adding to hea-bench

The four rules currently in hea-bench, namely the entropy rule, the size-mismatch rule, the VEC rule, and Î©, all capture orthogonal aspects of phase formation but none of them directly compare the Gibbs free energy of a hypothetical solid solution to that of the most stable competing intermetallic. King Î¦ does exactly this comparison, and Ye Ï adds a packing and size misfit contribution to the entropy term that Î© ignores. Adding one or both raises the descriptor count from six to seven or eight and adds a meaningfully different decision rule to the rules layer, since the existing four rules either consider entropy in isolation, size in isolation, valence electrons in isolation, or a simple Î© ratio that uses neither S_E nor a binary intermetallic reference.

### A.3 Conceptual comparison

King Î¦ and Ye Ï are not reformulations of one another. They share a symbol and the goal of predicting single-phase formation but differ in inputs, normalization, and threshold. King Î¦ requires explicit binary subsystem enumeration to find the most stable intermetallic or most segregated configuration. Ye Ï requires only multicomponent ÎH_mix, ÎS_mix, T_m, and the mismatch entropy S_E. King Î¦ produces a unitless number near unity. Ye Ï produces a unitless number near twenty. King Î¦ matches more closely the physical question "would the solid solution beat the best binary intermetallic" while Ye Ï matches more closely the question "is the entropic stabilization energy large compared to the enthalpic plus mismatch penalty." Treating them as two separate descriptors and two separate rules is the cleaner integration choice, and is the approach this plan recommends.

## B. Descriptor Layer Integration

### B.1 New descriptors

Two new scalar descriptors should be added to the descriptor component, alongside the existing six. Recommended attribute names that match hea-bench's existing snake_case style:

```
phi_king        : float    # Î¦ from King 2016
phi_ye          : float    # Ï from Ye 2015
```

Two intermediate quantities are needed and should also be exposed because they are useful in their own right and because exposing them keeps the public API testable:

```
delta_h_ss      : float    # enthalpy of disordered solid solution
delta_g_ss      : float    # Gibbs free energy of disordered solid solution at T
delta_g_max     : float    # extremal binary subsystem Gibbs energy
s_excess        : float    # Mansoori mismatch entropy S_E
```

Both phi computations should accept a temperature parameter T with a documented default. Two reasonable default policies are a fixed annealing temperature in the spirit of Troparevsky et al. (Phys. Rev. X 5, 011041, 2015), who used T_ann = 1000 K as a uniform value for all alloys, and a compositional rule such as T = 0.6 Â· T_m, which approximates the typical annealing fraction of the melting point used in many empirical HEA studies. The recommendation is to expose both as named policies and to default to T_ann = 1000 K, since this is a well-defined value drawn from a primary source rather than a rule of thumb.

### B.2 New data tables and licensing

**For King Î¦.** The pair enthalpy table already in hea-bench (the Miedema pair table from matminer, BSD-3-Clause) supplies the ÎH for arbitrary binary subsystems and is sufficient for the ÎH_ss term. The harder part is ÎG_max, which requires the formation enthalpy of the most stable intermetallic in each constituent binary. There are two practical options.

Option B.2.a. Approximate ÎG_max for each binary using the same Miedema framework, taking the most negative compound formation enthalpy that Miedema yields for that binary. This requires no new tables and keeps the dependency-free core. King and coworkers used essentially this approach in their original web service.

Option B.2.b. Replace the Miedema approximation with a table of measured or DFT-computed intermetallic formation enthalpies. Open candidates include the Materials Project (CC BY 4.0), the OQMD (CC BY 4.0), and the AFLOW repository (open data). Each of these has near-complete coverage of binary intermetallics for the 24 elements in hea-bench's property table, but introduces a network or vendored-data dependency that conflicts with the dependency-free core principle.

The recommendation is to ship Option B.2.a as the default in v0.2.0 and document Option B.2.b as an opt-in extension that users can enable through an optional extra, for instance `pip install hea-bench[dft-enthalpies]`. The license of any such opt-in table must be checked against MIT compatibility, and only data released under MIT, BSD, Apache-2.0, or CC BY compatible terms should be vendored.

**For Ye Ï.** The Mansoori mismatch entropy S_E requires only the atomic radii and a packing fraction. Atomic radii are already in the 24-element property table. The packing fraction Î¾ is a constant (0.68 or 0.74) and does not need to be tabulated. No new data table is required for Ye Ï.

### B.3 Changes to the existing 24-element property table

No new per-element columns are strictly required for either phi parameter. The columns already present, namely atomic radius, melting temperature, valence electrons, plus the Miedema pair table, are sufficient to compute both King Î¦ (under Option B.2.a) and Ye Ï. If Option B.2.b is adopted, the binary intermetallic formation enthalpies live in a separate table, not in the per-element table.

A minor side-channel change worth considering is adding an optional per-element Miedema parameter column for users who want to extend the library to elements outside the current 24. The King paper itself supports 73 elements. Keeping the current 24-element scope is the recommended initial choice to minimize the risk of coverage drift between descriptors.

### B.4 Coverage implications

The current coverage figure of 86.7 percent on the 7784-alloy benchmark is set by the element coverage of the property table, with the uncovered fraction dominated by alloys containing Mg, C, Zn, B, Sn, Re, Au, and Li. Because both phi parameters use the same underlying element properties and the same Miedema pair table as the existing descriptors, coverage for King Î¦ and Ye Ï should match the current 86.7 percent exactly. Adding either parameter does not improve and should not degrade coverage.

If Option B.2.b is later adopted and elements outside the present 24 are added, coverage rises mechanically, but that change is out of scope for the v0.2.0 phi integration and should be considered a separate v0.3.0 enhancement.

### B.5 Naming conventions

hea-bench's existing descriptor API uses lowercase snake_case attributes and Greek letter free names (`delta_s_mix`, `delta_h_mix`, `delta`, `vec`, `tm`, `omega`). The new descriptors should follow the same convention, with `phi_king` and `phi_ye` as the public attribute names. Documentation and docstrings should make explicit that `phi_king` corresponds to the capital Î¦ in the literature and `phi_ye` to the lowercase Ï, and should warn that the two are not equivalent despite the shared pronunciation.

## C. Rules Layer Integration

### C.1 Two new rules

The cleanest integration is to add two new rules to the rules layer, parallel to the existing four, each wrapping its descriptor with a tunable threshold.

```
rule_phi_king(alloy, T=None, threshold=1.0)  -> classification
rule_phi_ye(alloy, threshold=20.0)           -> classification
```

Each rule should expose the same classifier interface as the existing four rules: a `predict` method returning a coarse phase label, a `score` method returning the underlying scalar descriptor, and a `threshold` attribute that can be reassigned at runtime to support the threshold sweep capability.

The temperature argument T for `rule_phi_king` should default to the policy chosen in section B.1, with T_ann = 1000 K as the recommended default and a documented option to switch to T = 0.6 Â· T_m. Ye Ï has no explicit temperature dependence in the Ye 2015 formulation because T appears only implicitly through the assumption that S_E is the relevant entropy at the temperature of interest. The Ye rule therefore needs no temperature argument.

### C.2 Default thresholds from the source papers

```
phi_king default threshold = 1.0   # King et al., Acta Mater. 104 (2016) 172
phi_ye   default threshold = 20.0  # Ye et al., Scripta Mater. 104 (2015) 53
```

Both thresholds should be defined as named module-level constants, not magic numbers inside the rule body, to keep them auditable and overridable.

### C.3 Output category mapping

This is the most consequential design decision in the rules layer. King Î¦ and Ye Ï both predict a binary outcome: solid solution versus intermetallic. The existing hea-bench coarse labels are BCC, FCC, HCP, and multi-phase. Intermetallic predictions from a phi rule do not map cleanly onto any of these four.

There are three viable mapping strategies.

Strategy C.3.a, map intermetallic to multi-phase. The phi rule classifies all `Î¦ < 1` alloys as multi-phase. This is the simplest mapping and reuses the existing label set without modification. The downside is that multi-phase in hea-bench currently mixes together "two solid solutions" and "solid solution plus intermetallic," which the phi rules should arguably distinguish.

Strategy C.3.b, introduce a new coarse label "intermetallic" and keep multi-phase reserved for solid solution mixtures. This preserves phi's physical content but requires a label set change that propagates through the benchmark, the statistics, the evaluation routine, and the HTML calculator.

Strategy C.3.c, expose the phi rules as binary classifiers (`solid_solution` vs `intermetallic`) and let the benchmark evaluation routine collapse intermetallic predictions onto multi-phase when scoring against the existing 7784-alloy benchmark.

The recommendation is Strategy C.3.c for the initial v0.2.0 integration. It preserves the original binary semantics of the phi rules, requires no benchmark relabeling, and leaves room to upgrade to Strategy C.3.b in a future release once the side-channel intermetallic labels in the source datasets have been audited. The phi rules should return `solid_solution` or `intermetallic` as their native output and a thin adapter layer should translate `intermetallic` to `multi-phase` only at the point of scoring against the coarse benchmark labels.

The fine-grained intermetallic labels already retained as side-channel columns in the benchmark should be used to construct an "intermetallic-aware" sub-benchmark for the phi rules specifically. This sub-benchmark would score `solid_solution` versus `intermetallic` directly without collapsing onto multi-phase, and would give a more honest picture of phi's performance.

## D. Benchmark and Evaluation Integration

### D.1 Scoring against the existing 7784-alloy benchmark

Because the phi rules emit predictions that adapt to the existing coarse labels through the C.3.c mapping, the existing 7784-alloy benchmark can score the new phi rules without modification to the dataset itself. The evaluation routine needs to learn one new behavior: that for the phi rules, a `solid_solution` prediction should be scored as correct if the benchmark label is any of BCC, FCC, or HCP, and an `intermetallic` prediction should be scored as correct if the benchmark label is `multi-phase`. This is a per-rule scoring policy and should be implemented as a small extension to the existing evaluation routine, not as a wholesale rewrite.

### D.2 Changes to the evaluation routine

The minimum changes are:
- Register the two new rules with the rule registry.
- Add per-rule scoring policies, with phi rules using the binary to coarse mapping described in C.3.c.
- Ensure the per-outcome statistics component, which already produces sensitivity, specificity, Youden index, and Wilson confidence intervals per outcome, treats `solid_solution` and `intermetallic` as the two outcomes for phi rules, while the four existing rules continue to use their original outcome sets.

No changes are needed to the loader code for the three source datasets, since the phi rules consume the same chemical formulas that the existing rules consume.

### D.3 Threshold sweep integration

The existing threshold sweep capability iterates over a one-dimensional grid of threshold values for each rule and reports the receiver operating curve and the threshold that maximizes the Youden index. For King Î¦, the sweep should cover roughly [0.5, 1.5] in increments of 0.01. For Ye Ï, the sweep should cover roughly [5, 50] in increments of 0.5. Both ranges bracket the published default thresholds with enough margin to expose any genuine optimum, and both should be configurable.

A two-dimensional sweep, for instance over (threshold, temperature) for King Î¦, is a natural extension and should be supported by parameterizing the sweep routine to accept additional axes. It is not strictly needed for v0.2.0 and can be deferred.

### D.4 Comparison reporting

The per-outcome statistics for the phi rules should appear in the standard output table alongside the four existing rules, with a clear column or row label indicating that the phi rules' outcomes are binary while the others are coarse label. A small note in the reporting documentation should explain that the apparent specificity advantage of binary classifiers in this kind of joint reporting is an artifact of having fewer outcomes, not a genuine performance advantage. The Wilson confidence intervals already produced by the statistics component handle the small sample case correctly and do not need modification.

## E. Cross-Verification Protocol

### E.1 Why held-out evaluation matters

This section is written for readers who have not previously worked with held-out evaluation. The intuition is straightforward. When you tune the threshold of a rule on the same dataset that you then use to report the rule's accuracy, the reported accuracy is biased upward, sometimes substantially. The threshold sweep finds the value that happens to work best on the particular alloys you tested it on, including the quirks and noise specific to those alloys. If a new alloy arrives tomorrow, the threshold optimized in this way will not perform as well on the new alloy as it did on the tuning set. This phenomenon is called overfitting to the tuning set, and the gap between the in-sample optimum and the true expected performance is called the optimism bias.

The remedy is to separate the alloys used for tuning the threshold from the alloys used for reporting performance. The two simplest practical schemes are a single train and test split, and k-fold cross-validation.

In a single train and test split, the 7784 alloys are partitioned once into, for example, 70 percent training and 30 percent test. Thresholds are swept on the training portion only. The threshold that maximizes the Youden index on training is then frozen and applied to the test portion. The accuracy reported is the accuracy on the test portion. The advantage is simplicity. The disadvantage is that the single split is itself a source of noise, and the reported number depends on the particular random partition.

In k-fold cross-validation, the 7784 alloys are partitioned into k roughly equal folds. For each fold, the other k-1 folds are used as training, the threshold is optimized on them, and the held-out fold is scored. The reported accuracy is the average of the k held-out fold accuracies, often with an associated standard error across folds. The advantage is that every alloy contributes to both training and evaluation, so the result depends less on a single random split. The disadvantage is that the whole procedure runs k times rather than once.

Both schemes give estimates of performance on previously unseen alloys, which is what the user of a phase prediction rule actually cares about. In-sample optima are useful for sanity checking and for reproducing published threshold values, but they should never be reported as the headline performance number.

### E.2 Recommended cross-verification design for phi and for all rules

The recommended protocol is stratified k-fold cross-validation with k = 5, stratified jointly by phase label and by source dataset.

Stratifying by phase label means that each fold contains roughly the same proportion of BCC, FCC, HCP, multi-phase, and intermetallic alloys as the full benchmark. This matters because the phase classes are imbalanced, and an unstratified random split can produce folds with very few HCP examples, which would give noisy held-out estimates for any rule whose performance differs between HCP and FCC.

Stratifying by source dataset means that each fold contains a roughly proportional mix of alloys from Peivaste et al. 2023, Borg et al. 2020, and Pei et al. 2020. Cross-source variability is one of the main sources of label disagreement in the 7784-alloy union, and folding without stratification by source risks training thresholds on one source and testing on a different source, which is a different and harder generalization question.

For comparison and as a sanity check, a fixed 70/30 train/test split using a documented random seed should also be supported. The k-fold result is the headline number. The single split result is for quick reproduction.

### E.3 Handling cross-source disagreements

The benchmark records roughly 100 cross-source label conflicts, about 1.3 percent of the 7784 unique compositions. These are alloys for which the three source datasets disagree on the phase label. There are three principled ways to handle them in the cross-verification protocol.

Approach E.3.a, exclude. Drop the conflicted alloys from the cross-verification entirely. This produces the cleanest signal but discards a small amount of data and biases the benchmark toward consensus alloys.

Approach E.3.b, majority vote. Use the most frequent label among the sources as the ground truth. This retains all alloys but treats the conflict as fully resolved, which it is not.

Approach E.3.c, double scoring. Include the conflicted alloys but report two numbers, one in which they are scored as correct if any source label matches the prediction, and one in which they are scored as correct only if every source label matches. The gap between these two numbers quantifies the irreducible cross-source uncertainty.

The recommendation is Approach E.3.c as the headline protocol, with Approach E.3.a available as a secondary mode for users who want a strict consensus only evaluation. Approach E.3.b is not recommended because it hides genuine uncertainty.

### E.4 Tradeoffs

Stratified k-fold cross-validation with k = 5 and source and label stratification is more conservative than the original in-sample threshold sweep and will generally produce lower headline accuracies than the in-sample optima already reported by hea-bench 0.1.0. This is the expected behavior and should be documented prominently in the release notes for v0.2.0. Users who compare v0.1.0 numbers to v0.2.0 numbers and observe a drop should understand that the drop is the removal of optimism bias, not a regression in the underlying rules.

## F. HTML Calculator Integration

The standalone HTML calculator at https://dfieser.github.io/hea-bench/ reimplements the same descriptors and rules in client-side JavaScript so that the page is self-contained. For the phi integration, the JavaScript side needs the following.

For Ye Ï, the calculator already has atomic radii and Tm inlined for the 24 elements and already computes ÎH_mix and ÎS_mix. Adding S_E requires inlining only the packing fraction constants and implementing the Mansoori formula in JavaScript. No new data table is required.

For King Î¦, the calculator needs either the Miedema pair table inlined as a JSON literal in the JavaScript bundle, which it likely already has for the Î© computation, or a routine to find the most stable binary intermetallic from that same pair table. ## G. Testing and Validation

### G.1 Sanity checks for canonical alloys

Following the pattern of the existing CoCrFeMnNi sanity-check tests in section 3.1 of the hea-bench paper, the test suite should add locked-value tests for the two new descriptors on a small set of canonical compositions.

For CoCrFeMnNi (Cantor alloy, equiatomic), expected values based on the source-paper conventions are approximately:

```
phi_king (CoCrFeMnNi, T = 1000 K) â 1.2 to 1.5
phi_ye   (CoCrFeMnNi)             â 30 to 50
```

These ranges are approximate because both depend on the exact choices made for the Miedema parameters, the radii, and the temperature. The test should lock the actual computed value to the fourth decimal place and use that locked value as a regression target, not as an absolute physical truth. The expected qualitative outcomes are that both rules predict `solid_solution` for CoCrFeMnNi, consistent with experimental observation.

Additional canonical alloys for sanity-check tests should include AlCoCrFeNi (mixed FCC plus BCC, both rules should predict near the boundary), CoCrFeNi (single-phase FCC, both rules should clearly predict solid_solution), and an alloy known to form intermetallics such as AlCoCrCuFeNi at a high Al fraction (both rules should predict intermetallic).

### G.2 Verifying implementation against the source papers

Two reproduction tests should be added. The first reproduces King and coworkers' reported 177 of 185 accuracy on their original 185-alloy benchmark. The 185 compositions and their phase outcomes are listed in the King 2016 supplementary information and should be vendored as a fixture file under a clearly licensed format. The test asserts that the implementation reproduces the published accuracy to within a small tolerance, allowing for minor differences in element coverage between hea-bench's 24 elements and King's 73 elements.

The second reproduces the Ye Ï values for the small set of alloys that Ye and coworkers tabulate in their Scripta Materialia paper. These values should be reproducible to within two significant figures using the same Miedema pair table and the same packing fraction conventions.

### G.3 Property based tests

In addition to fixed value tests, two property based tests are useful. The first checks that Ï_Ye is monotonically increasing in ÎS_mix for fixed ÎH_mix, S_E, and Tm. The second checks that Î¦_King decreases when the most negative binary intermetallic enthalpy is made more negative, all other quantities held fixed. Both properties follow directly from the definitions and should hold for any correct implementation.

## I. Risks, Limitations, and Open Questions

### I.1 Data availability and licensing

The Miedema pair table from matminer is BSD-3-Clause and compatible with hea-bench's MIT license. If Option B.2.b is later adopted and intermetallic formation enthalpies from a DFT database are vendored, the license of that database must be confirmed compatible. Materials Project data are released under CC BY 4.0, which is compatible. OQMD data have a similar permissive license. Care is needed because some commercial DFT databases ship data under restrictive terms that are not compatible with MIT redistribution.

### I.2 Element coverage

Both phi parameters in their Option B.2.a form rely on the same 24-element property table and the same Miedema pair table as the existing descriptors, so element coverage is identical at 86.7 percent. The 13.3 percent of the benchmark dominated by Mg, C, Zn, B, Sn, Re, Au, and Li remains uncovered. Expanding the property table to cover these elements is a separate work item.

### I.3 Mapping intermetallic to multi-phase

The Strategy C.3.c mapping treats `intermetallic` predictions from a phi rule as predictions of `multi-phase` for scoring purposes. This is a deliberate simplification that loses information. Alloys whose true microstructure is solid solution plus intermetallic are scored the same as alloys whose true microstructure is two solid solutions, even though a phi rule that distinguishes these would be more useful. The recommended remedy is the intermetallic aware sub-benchmark described in section C.3, scored separately from the main benchmark.

### I.4 Known failure modes

The published literature documents two recurring failure modes for both phi parameters. The first is alloys containing Al, where ordered B2 or L12 phases are conventionally counted as solid solutions in the HEA community but are flagged as intermetallics by both phi rules. The second is alloys with strong short-range order that are formally disordered solid solutions on a long range basis but behave thermodynamically more like partially ordered intermetallics. These failure modes are not bugs in the phi parameters but limitations of the binary solid solution versus intermetallic abstraction. They should be acknowledged in the documentation and, where possible, flagged as caveats in the per-alloy output of the rule.

### I.5 Open questions

Three open questions remain for future work. First, whether a temperature aware version of Ye Ï would improve its performance, since the original formulation does not expose T as a tunable input. Second, whether the King Î¦ â¥ 1 threshold should be allowed to vary by alloy family, since the published threshold is a single value across all families. Third, whether the combination of King Î¦ and Ye Ï as a two rule ensemble outperforms either rule alone, which is a natural question that the v0.2.0 evaluation routine can answer directly.

## J. Suggested Implementation Phasing

The implementation should proceed in seven phases, each with an explicit success criterion that gates the next phase.

**Phase 1: data tables and constants.** Confirm that the Miedema pair table and the 24-element property table are sufficient under Option B.2.a, add the packing fraction constants and the default temperature policy as module level named constants, and decide on the public attribute names. Success criterion: a frozen data layer with no compute logic, reviewed and merged.

**Phase 2: descriptor implementation.** Add `phi_king`, `phi_ye`, and the four exposed intermediates (`delta_h_ss`, `delta_g_ss`, `delta_g_max`, `s_excess`) to the descriptor component. Success criterion: descriptor values for CoCrFeMnNi, CoCrFeNi, and AlCoCrFeNi match expected ranges and are locked into regression tests.

**Phase 3: rule implementation.** Add `rule_phi_king` and `rule_phi_ye` to the rules component, with default thresholds 1.0 and 20.0 respectively, and the Strategy C.3.c output mapping. Success criterion: both rules return the expected `solid_solution` or `intermetallic` classification for the canonical alloys in phase 2.

**Phase 4: evaluation and statistics integration.** Register the two new rules with the evaluation routine, implement the binary to coarse scoring policy, and add the phi rules to the per-outcome statistics output. Success criterion: end to end run on the 7784-alloy benchmark produces a coherent statistics table that includes the two new rules alongside the existing four.

**Phase 5: cross-verification protocol.** Implement stratified 5-fold cross-validation stratified jointly by phase label and source dataset, and add the conflict handling modes from section E.3. Success criterion: held-out accuracies and confidence intervals for all six rules, with the optimism gap between in-sample and held-out clearly visible and reported.

**Phase 6: HTML calculator and tests.** Update the JavaScript implementation to mirror the Python descriptors and rules, add cross-implementation parity tests, and add the property based tests from section G.3. Success criterion: JavaScript and Python implementations agree on all canonical alloys to within a small floating-point tolerance, and property based tests pass.

**Phase 7: documentation and release.** Update the README, the paper style description, and the release notes, then tag v0.2.0. Success criterion: a clean release with a changelog that names the two new rules, the cross-verification protocol, and the intermetallic mapping decision, and that warns users about the headline accuracy change relative to v0.1.0.

The full phasing is expected to require modest effort because the data layer and the rules architecture of hea-bench are already well factored for this kind of addition.
